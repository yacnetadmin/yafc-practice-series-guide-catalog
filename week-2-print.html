Week Two: Being and Doing

"The Church's approach to an intelligent carpenter is usually confined to exhorting him not to be drunk and disorderly in his leisure hours, and to come to church on Sundays. What the Church should be telling him is this: that the very first demand that his religion makes upon him is that he should make good tables. Church by all means, and decent forms of amusement, certainly—but what use is all that if in the very center of his life and occupation he is insulting God with bad carpentry? No crooked table legs or ill-fitting drawers ever came out of the carpenter's shop at Nazareth. Nor, if they did, could anyone believe that they were made by the same hand that made Heaven and earth." Dorothy Sayers, Why Work?

Western Christianity, traced back to its near Eastern roots in the 4th century, has either explicitly or more often implicitly taught and modeled a divide between the sacred and the secular. In simple terms, vocations that were explicitly "Christian" such as pastoral work, mission work, parachurch ministry, etc. were seen as both somehow holier and more worthwhile than other types of work. The unintended consequence of this reality has been that followers of Jesus see just a few hours each week as times that they can explicitly bring glory to Jesus. The biblical narrative, however, tells a very different story.

Paul reminds the Corinthian church that "Whether you eat or drink or whatever you do, do it all to the glory of God." (1 Corinthians 10:31) According to Genesis 1-2, work was given to humans as a gift prior to sin entering the world, and by stepping into our identity and calling, we join God in His creative work in the world and thereby redeem work.

Questions for Community

  1. Do you see your work as a gift from God? Why or why not? Do you see your work as a way to glorify God? Why or why not?
  2. Have someone read Ephesians 2:10. How might meditating on that verse each morning change the way you live during the day?

Individual Practice

This practice is adapted from "Habit Audit" by Josh Porter.

This week you are invited to perform what is called a "habit audit" of your life rhythms and habits in order to better understand what you love and, thus, what is forming you. Keep a short and simple diary of your habits and patterns of life each day this week. You can keep a small notebook on your person for the week or use digital journal. Make note of the amount of time spent on each activity. Take particular note of the things to which you commit time, energy, and money.

Excerpts from such a diary may read something like:

  • 15 minutes making coffee
  • 30 minutes on social media
  • 1 hour working out
  • 2 hours of TV shows
  • 2 hours and $30 at a restaurant with friends
  • 1 hour and $25 shopping online

When the week and diary is complete, ask yourself a few questions.

  • What rituals and habits are shaping me?
  • What has been shaping me without my knowing?
  • Are the things to which I dedicate my time leading me to Jesus?
